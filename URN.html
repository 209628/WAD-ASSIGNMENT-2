Uniform Resource Locator ｜ URI

URN

Definition:

- It is a type of URI that is used to identify a resource by its unique and permanent name rather than by its location.

- Unlike a URL which tells you where to find a resource on the internet a URN gives the resource a name that remains constant and never changes even if the resource is moved to a different location or the website goes offline.

- This makes URNs very useful for identifying resources that need to have a permanent and stable identity regardless of where they are stored or accessed.

Structure of a URI:

- A URN follows a specific structure that consists of three main parts.

  • The first part is the scheme which is always written as urn: to indicate that it is a URN
  • The second part is the namespace identifier which specifies the category or type of the resource such as isbn for books or issn for magazines and journals
  • The third part is the namespace specific string which is the unique identifier for that specific resource within its namespace such as the actual ISBN number of a book

- The most common and well known example of a URN is the ISBN (International Standard Book Number) which is used to uniquely identify books; every book published in the world has its own unique ISBN number that never changes regardless of where the book is sold or stored.

- Another example is the ISSN (International Standard Serial Number) which is used to identify magazines, journals and other periodical publications.
